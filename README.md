# SECTION 1 — The Brazilian Football Broadcasting Rights Market

Brazilian football is one of the most globally followed sports leagues — yet its broadcasting rights market has, for most of its existence, operated under remarkably closed conditions. **Our goal in this section is to bring clarity to a market that is in the middle of a structural transition** — one where regulatory unbundling, institutional capital, and digital disruption are simultaneously rewriting economics that had been stable for three decades.

**In what follows, we trace the trajectory from how Brazilian football ended up so closed in the first place, through the regulatory reforms that broke it open, to the commercial architecture that now defines the market.** We begin in 1987, with the formation of the Clube dos 13, and walk through the decade-long unravelling that culminated in the 2021 Lei do Mandante. From there, we introduce the two competing aggregators that emerged in 2022 — Libra and LFU — follow their divergent trajectories through 2025, and describe the CBF-mediated unification process now underway. We close by mapping the 2025-2029 contract architecture across five broadcasters and the rules governing how the resulting revenue gets distributed among the clubs.

The story, in short, is one of moving from monopoly to fragmentation — and now potentially back to consolidation, but on radically different terms than before. That said, the story is still being written, and the 2029 renegotiation cycle will be the moment of truth. The framework below is designed to help investors orient themselves ahead of that inflection.

# Pillar #1 — From Unanimity to Fragmentation (1987-2020)

**Brazilian football is a national passion, but as a business it has long been one of the most concentrated television markets in the world.** For more than three decades, the rights to broadcast Brazil's top professional league — the Brasileirão (Brazilian Championship, Série A) — were sold collectively by a single bloc of clubs, and bought almost exclusively by a single broadcaster, Globo (Brazil's largest media group). To understand why the market is changing now, we need to start with how that closed system was built — and how it began to crack.

### Clube dos 13 — origin and collective era

**The story begins on 11 July 1987, when Brazil's thirteen leading football clubs formed a negotiating cartel called the Clube dos 13** (formally, the União dos Grandes Clubes do Futebol Brasileiro — "Union of the Major Brazilian Football Clubs"). The goal was simple: instead of each club negotiating individually with broadcasters, they would sell their TV rights together as a single package, giving them collective bargaining power against the country's dominant TV network. The strategy worked. The first multi-year deal with Globo, signed in 1997, covered the 1997-1999 cycle. By the 2009-2011 cycle, the contract had grown to roughly BRL 1.4bn — the high-water mark of the clubs' collective pricing power. **In our view**, the C13 era succeeded not because Brazilian football was uniquely concentrated, but because the legal regime made unilateral selling impractical — a structural feature that would later become the very lever for fragmentation.

### The unravelling (2011-2012)

**The 2011-2012 cycle was when the model began to fall apart.** The biggest clubs in the country — Flamengo, Vasco, Fluminense, Botafogo, Santos and Palmeiras — decided they could get better terms by negotiating directly with Globo, rather than splitting the collective pot equally with smaller clubs. They walked out of the Clube dos 13 one by one. Without its anchor members, the cartel lost its leverage and was effectively dissolved over the next decade (it survived only formally, to handle residual legal matters). The exits were individually rational — each big club got more money — but they structurally weakened the position of the clubs left behind. **Importantly**, the exits were a classic prisoner's dilemma: each defection was rational individually but value-destroying for the group. We believe the same dynamic now threatens both Libra and LFU at the margins — which is why the unification process in Pillar #4 matters disproportionately for the smaller clubs that have the most to lose if the blocs fragment further.

### The decade of effective Globo monopsony (2012-2020)

**For roughly ten years after the Clube dos 13 collapsed, the market settled into an unwritten arrangement: Globo as the only meaningful buyer of football rights across every distribution channel.** Globo controlled free-to-air broadcast TV (the open Globo channel), pay-TV (its subscription cable channel SporTV) and pay-per-view (Premiere, its premium package for individual matches). There were no other buyers with the scale to challenge it. Pricing power sat firmly with Globo. Importantly, this is the structural backdrop that the upcoming regulatory reform would disrupt — and, in our view, the mental model that still anchors many investors' view of Brazilian football economics today.

# Pillar #2 — Regulatory Triggers (2020-2021)

**The reason Globo's dominance lasted so long was not just commercial — it was legal.** Under Brazilian law, the rights to broadcast a football match (called direitos de arena, or arena rights) were jointly held by the home club and the visiting club. In practice, this meant any broadcaster wanting to show a game had to get both clubs to agree, which made fragmented or individual selling impractical. The simplest solution was to negotiate with the bloc and Globo at the same time — which is exactly what happened for thirty years. Two regulatory changes between 2020 and 2021 broke this structure, laying the legal foundation for everything that followed.

### MP 984/2020 — the failed first attempt

**In June 2020, the Bolsonaro government issued an emergency decree called MP 984 (Medida Provisória, or Provisional Measure) that transferred arena rights exclusively to the home club.** The decree did one thing, and one thing only: it removed the visiting club from the equation. This amended Brazil's main sports laws (the Lei Pelé of 1998 and the Statute of the Fan of 2003). However, in Brazil emergency decrees expire automatically if not voted into permanent law by Congress within 120 days — and MP 984 lapsed without a vote, reverting the rules back to the previous regime. That said, the attempt established the legal principle and built the political momentum for the definitive legislation that came a year later. **In our view**, MP 984's failure was less about substance and more about timing — the Bolsonaro-Congress relationship in mid-2020 was strained on multiple fronts, and arena rights was not the political priority needed to overcome legislative inertia within the 120-day window.

### Law 14,205/2021 (Lei do Mandante) — the definitive change

**In September 2021, Congress passed Law 14,205 — informally known as the Lei do Mandante ("Home Club Law") — and the structure changed permanently.** Arena rights were transferred to the home club for every match in Brazil, with one carve-out: athletes (including reserves) are entitled to 5% of arena rights revenue, distributed equally. The practical effect was transformative. From that moment, broadcasters needed to negotiate with only one club per match, and individual clubs gained the unilateral right to sell their own games — either alone, or in self-organised groups (blocs) of their choosing. **The collective monopoly was no longer a legal necessity; it was now a voluntary choice.** **The key implication for investors**: the new rule did not just enable bloc formation — it also enabled bloc fragmentation. Clubs can leave any bloc at any time and sell their games elsewhere, which is the structural reason Libra and LFU have been losing members since 2024 and why we believe the path to stability runs through unification rather than competition.

### Why this matters in practice

**The first national championship played fully under the new regime was the 2025 Brasileirão — and it was also the first to show the regulatory change materialised in contract architecture.** The shift turned a monopoly into a multi-buyer landscape: with home clubs holding unilateral selling rights, the new blocs (Libra and LFU, which we describe next) could aggregate and negotiate competing packages — attracting Amazon, Record (a free-to-air TV network) and CazéTV/YouTube as new entrants alongside the incumbent Globo group.

# Pillar #3 — Libra and LFU: The Two Blocs

**Once the law changed in 2021, Brazilian clubs faced a structural choice: sell their rights individually, or organise into new collective blocs better suited to the new rules.** The answer came quickly. Two competing leagues emerged in rapid succession during 2022 — both within the same calendar year, both representing fundamentally different philosophies about how to structure the new market. Libra came first, built around concentration and the financial weight of Brazil's biggest clubs. LFU came second, built around egalitarian distribution and broader club inclusion. The two blocs spent 2022-2025 in open competition for member clubs and rights deals. As Pillar #4 will show, they are now negotiating to merge — but the journey to that point is the story of how Brazilian football was reorganised. **This pillar tells the full story of how each bloc came together, who is currently in each, and what their internal politics look like today.**

### The pre-history: why two blocs emerged

**The collapse of the Clube dos 13 in 2011-2012 left a vacuum, but the Lei do Mandante (2021) created the urgency to fill it.** By early 2022, two camps had formed among the country's top clubs. The first, led by Flamengo, Corinthians and other heavyweights, wanted a league modelled on the Premier League — one where the biggest clubs commanded the biggest share of revenue, with distribution rules that rewarded brand size and historical performance. The second camp, comprising mostly mid-tier and rising clubs, wanted a more egalitarian model that protected the relevance and economics of clubs outside the traditional "big four". Both groups attempted to negotiate a unified solution through late 2021 and early 2022, but the difference in distribution philosophy proved irreconcilable. By May 2022, the two sides separated — leading to the parallel creation of Libra and (shortly after) LFU.

### Libra — from 3 May 2022 to today

**Libra (Liga do Futebol Brasileiro) was formally founded on 3 May 2022 by eight clubs:** Corinthians, Cruzeiro, Flamengo, Palmeiras, Ponte Preta, Red Bull Bragantino, São Paulo and Santos. The bloc was modelled explicitly on the Premier League, with the stated goal of distributing TV revenues more efficiently — which in the founders' view meant more weight to commercial size. At its peak, Libra grew to 18 clubs throughout 2022-2024. **However, the bloc has suffered serial defections since.** Of the original 8 founders, only Flamengo, Red Bull Bragantino, São Paulo and Santos remain. The new members are Bahia, Grêmio, Vitória, Remo, Paysandu, Volta Redonda, ABC, Guarani, Sampaio Corrêa and Brusque. For the 2026 Brasileirão Série A, Libra is expected to field 10 clubs — down from 12 in 2025 and the original 18 ambition. **In our view**, Libra's defection trajectory reflects a Premier League aspiration meeting a Brazilian reality: the country's top clubs are concentrated enough to dominate the audience component every year, which means smaller Libra members systematically receive less than what they would in a more egalitarian bloc.

### LFU/FFU — from 28 June 2022 through multiple rebrands

**LFU emerged as the direct counterpoint to Libra, and its history is the more complex of the two.** The bloc was formalised on 28 June 2022 — just 56 days after Libra — by 25 clubs opposed to Libra's distribution proposal, originally under the name "Liga Forte Futebol" (LFF). It went through two rebrands: in July 2023, after Botafogo, Coritiba, Cruzeiro and Vasco formed a sub-bloc called "Grupo União", the league was renamed "Liga Forte União" (LFU); the most recent rebrand in 2025-2026 produced **"Futebol Forte União" (FFU)** — a positioning shift signalling a more permanent commercial league identity. The institutional capital base was built across 2022-2024 (detailed in the next sub-section). As of mid-2026, FFU operates with 10 clubs in Série A — Athletico-PR, Atlético-GO, Botafogo, Corinthians, Criciúma, Cruzeiro, Cuiabá, Fortaleza, Internacional and Juventude — plus Fluminense and Vasco. The broader membership includes LFF (21 clubs) and Grupo União (4 clubs) across Séries A, B and C. **That said**, we believe FFU's strategic positioning has structural limits: its egalitarian formula attracts mid-tier clubs but caps the bloc's growth ceiling, because the country's largest clubs are systematically worse off under those rules. FFU has won the 2025-2029 contract-value race, but it cannot easily attract Libra's anchor members in a future cycle without changing the formula — which would, in turn, alienate the current base.

### The diverging trajectory — LFU vs Libra side by side

**The two blocs took opposite paths from their parallel May/June 2022 birth.** LFU consistently added members and grew its economic position; Libra has lost members in every major realignment since 2023. Looking at the dates and club counts side-by-side makes the contrast unmistakable.

**LFU/FFU's strengthening trajectory:**

- **Jul/2022** — Constitution with 23 clubs across Séries A, B and C
- **Jul/2023** — Closing of LFU rebrand with 26 clubs (formation of Grupo União with Botafogo, Cruzeiro, Vasco, Coritiba)
- **Nov/2023** — Agreement with Paulistas clubs maintains 26-club count
- **May/2024** — Closing of Série A rights sale: BRL 1.1bn disbursed by investors (Serengeti, Life Capital Partners, XP); 31 clubs
- **Jul/2024** — Agreement with Corinthians (migrating from Libra); 32 clubs
- **Jan/2025** — LFU reaches 80% of Série B clubs; 33 clubs total
- **Sep/2025** — Agreement with Vitória; LFU reaches 70% of combined Séries A+B clubs (34 clubs), with economic position +50% versus 2024

**Libra's shrinking trajectory:**

- **May/2022** — Constitution with 18 clubs
- **Apr/2023** — Mubadala proposal announced; 21 clubs at peak
- **Jul/2023** — Exit of Botafogo, Cruzeiro, Vasco (joining LFU's Grupo União); drops to 19 clubs
- **Mar/2024** — Closing of Série A sale with Globo at BRL 1.3bn/year; 23 clubs
- **May-Jul/2025** — Exit of Grupo União-aligned clubs and further attrition; 17 clubs
- **Sep/2025** — Flamengo dispute settled with incremental BRL 150mn over 4 years; 17 clubs remain

**The pattern is striking.** LFU grew by ~50% in members and ~50% in economic position over three years; Libra shrunk by 7 clubs from its 2024 peak and is now in active dispute with its largest remaining member. **In our view**, the trajectory tells investors that the bloc-vs-bloc competition is effectively over — and that the unification process in Pillar #4 is the natural conclusion of a contest that LFU has, for practical purposes, already won.

### Institutional capital and cross-holdings

**LFU's most consequential structural move was attracting institutional capital.** The transaction was completed in stages across 2023-2024 and resulted in the sale of 10-20% of the bloc's commercial rights for 50 years for BRL 2.6bn — initially structured at 20% in the Serengeti/Life Capital Partners deal of June 2023, then renegotiated to 10% in 2024 before final payment. The anchor investors today are Life Capital Partners (a Brazilian sports-focused private equity firm), XP Private Equity, and General Atlantic (a US-based global growth equity firm). General Atlantic's commitment was completed in April 2024 alongside its parallel investment in LiveMode — the company that intermediates LFU's broadcasting deals. **Importantly, the cross-holding is structurally relevant**: the same fund backs both the seller (LFU) and the distribution platform (LiveMode), creating a vertical alignment between league capital, distribution platform and rights buyers that is unusual in global sports media. We develop the LiveMode side of this in Section 2.

**Transaction comparables anchor the LFU valuation.** Internal LFU materials position the BRL 1.3bn / 10% / 50-year deal against three reference points from global sports private equity, all structured on similar 50-year horizons:

- **Reference deal #1**: BRL 2.4bn at 10% — implies 11.5x 2023 revenue
- **Reference deal #2**: BRL 11.2bn at 10% — implies 12.2x 2023 revenue
- **Reference deal #3**: BRL 14.1bn at 13% — implies 14.1x 2023 revenue

LFU's implied multiple in its closing transaction falls within this range when measured against the bloc's 2023 commercial rights revenue base. **In our view**, the comparables LFU referenced are likely drawn from CVC's 2021 investment in La Liga (€2bn for ~8% over 50 years), Silver Lake's adjacent positions in F1 and the Manchester City Football Group, and Saudi PIF flows into other premium sports rights — all of them established at similar revenue multiples and similar long-dated horizons. **The takeaway**: LFU was priced as a real, institutional-grade sports rights vehicle, not as a one-off private deal — which tells us that General Atlantic, XP and Life Capital Partners saw the transaction as comparable to global precedent rather than as Brazilian-specific opportunism, and that the multiple is defensible if the unified-league hypothesis (Pillar #4) materialises.

### Mubadala-Libra discussions

**Libra has its own institutional capital story, but it remains unfinished.** Mubadala — the Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund — has been in extended discussions with Libra throughout 2024-2025. The original proposal envisioned a BRL 4bn injection, conditional on at least 18-team participation. The subsequent reformulated proposal scaled the ambition back: a 50/50 commercialisation agency (Libra clubs and Mubadala) with a BRL 1.4bn guaranteed annual revenue floor, contingent on at least 9 Libra clubs in Série A. The agency would hold exclusive selling rights for 25 years (2025-2049). That said, the proposal remains under discussion with no binding commitment — and the unification dynamics described in Pillar #4 may render the original Mubadala thesis structurally obsolete. **In our view**, the original Mubadala thesis is structurally challenged at current terms: the deal was conceived for an 18-team Libra, and the bloc is now operating at 10 teams with further exits possible. Mubadala would more plausibly re-enter at the unified-league level — which is a different transaction structurally.

### Realignments and the Flamengo dispute

**Throughout 2024-2025, the composition of the two blocs shifted dramatically.** Corinthians migrated from Libra to LFU in late 2024. Atlético-MG followed, with Neofeed reporting the move could catalyse further exits from Libra. The most consequential event came in late 2025, when Flamengo (the largest club in Brazil by fan base and revenue) entered into a public dispute with the rest of Libra. Under new president "Bap" (Luiz Eduardo Baptista), Flamengo argued that the audience-calculation methodology embedded in Libra's distribution rules lacked technical clarity — and was unfairly underweighting its share. The eventual settlement gave Flamengo an incremental BRL 150mn over four years on top of the standard distribution, paid in annual instalments of BRL 37.5mn with inflation indexation from 2027. **Palmeiras announced its exit from Libra immediately afterwards, citing "selfish attitudes" from the remaining members.** Notably, these realignments are not yet over: several sources have indicated that further migrations could occur when the current rights cycle ends in 2029.

### CADE oversight — the regulatory backdrop

**In late 2025, CADE (Brazil's competition authority) chancelled both Libra and LFU agreements but imposed conditions.** Libra paid a BRL 559k fine for "gun-jumping" — failing to notify CADE before consolidating commercial rights. CADE then barred both blocs from adding new clubs during an ongoing investigation into potential anticompetitive behaviour. That said, this regulatory pressure is now part of the structural backdrop: it limits bloc growth in the short term, and the eventual outcome of the CADE investigation will materially shape the regulatory perimeter of the unified league described in Pillar #4. **Our base case**: CADE eventually approves the unified league with binding conditions on minority-club protections and revenue-distribution caps. **The principal risk** is timing — if CADE acts before the unification process concludes, we believe it could disrupt the negotiation by setting external boundaries that one of the blocs would otherwise resist.

### The SAF revolution — investor-led clubs reshape bloc dynamics

**Parallel to the Lei do Mandante, Congress passed Law 14,193/2021 — the "Lei da SAF" — which allowed Brazilian football clubs to convert from non-profit associations into corporations (Sociedade Anônima do Futebol).** This was a structural change in club governance and financing: clubs could now issue shares, attract institutional capital, and operate under standard corporate law instead of association rules. As of 2026, **42 Brazilian clubs have already converted to SAFs**, with investors ranging from family offices to global private equity. The implication for bloc dynamics is direct — SAFs answer to their investors, who care about commercial maximisation rather than legacy loyalty to one bloc or another.

The most consequential transactions:

- **Red Bull Bragantino (2019) — Red Bull GmbH.** The earliest precursor, predating the Lei da SAF by two years. Red Bull's acquisition of Clube Atlético Bragantino (Bragança Paulista) was structured under earlier corporate frameworks rather than the formal SAF model, but operated like one in substance: single corporate owner, professional management, and full integration with Red Bull's global multi-club portfolio (Salzburg, Leipzig, New York Red Bulls). Widely cited as the proof-of-concept that paved the way for Law 14,193/2021. Bragantino is one of the four original Libra founders that remained in the bloc.
- **Botafogo SAF (2022) — John Textor / Eagle Football.** US-American investor paid a minimum of BRL 400mn for control; Textor also owns Lyon (France), Crystal Palace (England, until 2024) and Molenbeek (Belgium). Botafogo's eventual move from Libra to LFU is widely read as an investor-driven decision rather than a traditional club one.
- **Cruzeiro SAF (2021) — Ronaldo → Pedro Lourenço (2024).** Ex-footballer Ronaldo Nazário took control for ~BRL 400mn; sold to Pedro Lourenço (owner of Supermercados BH) in 2024 amid debt rebuild. Cruzeiro is in LFU.
- **Vasco SAF (2022) — 777 Partners.** BRL 1.4bn deal that became the cautionary tale of the SAF era. 777 imploded globally in 2024 (multiple US lawsuits, parallel collapses at other 777-controlled clubs), Brazilian courts suspended its rights at Vasco, and the club temporarily reverted to associative control while a successor investor is sought.
- **Bahia SAF (2023) — City Football Group.** Manchester City's parent group acquired control — the only Brazilian SAF backed by an established global football operator.
- **Atlético-MG SAF (2024).** Recent conversion with concentrated local ownership rather than external PE; the club ended 2025 with rising debt despite the new structure.

**In our view, the SAF wave matters to the broadcasting story for three reasons.** First, it brought professional investors into bloc-level negotiations — the GA-LFU-LiveMode triangle (developed in Section 2) only makes sense in a market where clubs are corporatised and can accept private capital. Second, it accelerated bloc-vs-bloc fragmentation, because SAF investors optimise for their own economics rather than honouring associative loyalties — which explains why migrations between blocs have clustered among recently-converted SAFs (Botafogo, Cruzeiro, Atlético-MG). Third, **it introduced execution risk**: the 777 collapse at Vasco, recurring debt issues at Atlético-MG and Botafogo, and the broader question of whether Brazilian football can sustain this many institutional investors simultaneously. **That said**, we believe the SAF wave is irreversible — the legal framework is established, the capital is already deployed, and the few large clubs that have resisted conversion (notably Flamengo and São Paulo) remain associations more by tradition than by strategic preference.

# Pillar #4 — Where It's Heading: Toward a Single League

**The natural endpoint of the trajectory described in Pillar #3 is unification.** By early 2026, both blocs had reached a state of strategic exhaustion. Libra had lost half of its membership and was struggling to attract institutional capital on competitive terms. LFU had won the deal-value race for the 2025-2029 cycle but recognised that further bloc-vs-bloc competition was eroding the total addressable pool. The CBF, under new president Samir Xaud (who took office in 2025), began actively mediating between the two sides. The result is a formal process now underway: a Memorandum of Understanding between Libra and LFU, a structured CBF-led timeline through 2026, and a stated target of operating a single unified league by 2030. **This pillar describes that process — what is on the table, who is driving it, what timeline is being followed, and what obstacles remain.**

### The MOU: two pillars of integration

**In late 2025 / early 2026, Libra and LFU exchanged a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)** — not yet a binding contract, but a structured agreement on the principles of integration. The MOU rests on two pillars. First, the two blocs commit to commercialising broadcasting rights jointly from a future cycle onwards, rather than competing for individual deals. Second, they commit to building a shared framework of rules and standards for the operational management of Séries A and B (encompassing competition format, financial regulation, infrastructure standards and other governance matters). The MOU explicitly does not include a definitive timeline — that is being developed in parallel through the CBF process — but it establishes the strategic direction. **In our view**, the MOU's signaling value exceeds its legal weight: it tells the market that both blocs have institutionally accepted that bloc-vs-bloc competition has run its course. The economic logic now favours one seller against multiple buyers — and the question is no longer whether to unify but how.

### The CBF's mediating role under Samir Xaud

**The CBF, under president Samir Xaud, has taken an active and unusual role in the unification process.** Historically, the CBF and the clubs have had an adversarial relationship over revenue distribution and competition format. Under Xaud, the CBF has positioned itself as facilitator rather than competitor: publicly supporting the creation of an independent league for Séries A and B, hosting meetings between the two blocs at its headquarters, and proposing operational principles for the unified league. Importantly, the CBF has indicated it will not seek to retain direct operational control of the Brasileirão — meaning Brazilian football's future league would be club-owned and club-managed, similar to the Premier League and La Liga. **We believe this trade is unambiguously favourable for the CBF**: it reduces operational complexity, eliminates the recurring revenue-distribution battles with clubs, and preserves the commercial pieces that matter most to the confederation (the national team rights, FIFA-level commercial relationships, and the calendar authority). The unusual cooperative stance is, in our view, the most underappreciated signal that unification is now base case rather than aspiration.

### The April 2026 meeting and the structured 2026 timeline

**The unification process took a critical step forward on 6 April 2026, when the CBF convened a meeting at its Barra da Tijuca headquarters in Rio de Janeiro with 38 of the 40 clubs across Séries A and B** (only Chapecoense and Mirassol were absent). The meeting laid out a structured timeline for the rest of 2026: from May to July, the blocs would collect suggestions and build initial proposals; from August to September, those proposals would be presented, adjusted and submitted for club approval; from October to December, the focus would shift to commercialisation structure and drafting the new league's statute. The expectation is that the new league's statute will be ready by end-2026, with operational improvements (joint marketing, joint regulatory framework) implementable before 2030.

### The target: a unified league operating from 2030

**The structural target is to launch the unified league at the start of the 2030 season, immediately following the end of the current 2025-2029 broadcasting cycle.** This timing is not coincidental — it allows the existing contracts to run their course before the new league assumes commercial operations. The unified league would absorb the rights commercialisation function from both Libra and LFU; manage the competition calendar for Séries A and B; impose a common set of governance, financial-fair-play and infrastructure rules across all member clubs; and negotiate as a single counterparty with broadcasters in the 2029 renegotiation. The model is consistent with the Premier League and La Liga, where independent leagues manage commercial rights under federation oversight.

### Obstacles to consolidation

**Despite progress, several obstacles remain.** The first is financial fair play. LFU has historically demanded stricter financial-fair-play rules, arguing that clubs that systematically overspend create unfair competition for those managing finances responsibly; Libra (and especially its largest member, Flamengo) has been more reluctant to accept binding spending rules that could limit its competitive flexibility. The second is the synthetic-pitch debate. Brazilian clubs are split on whether synthetic pitches should be allowed in Brasileirão matches, with some smaller clubs (including those in LFU) viewing synthetic surfaces as an essential cost-saving measure, while larger clubs prefer to mandate natural-grass standards. The third is governance: how voting rights are allocated, whether all clubs have equal weight, and whether minority members can be outvoted on substantive decisions. The April 2026 meeting did not resolve any of these — they remain on the table for the May-September negotiation phase. **In our view**, financial fair play is the genuine blocker; the synthetic-pitch debate is symbolic and easier to compromise. If the unification stalls, we believe the FFP/Flamengo dynamic will be the proximate cause — and that is the single most consequential issue we are monitoring through Q3 2026.

### Why the 2029 renegotiation is the inflection point

**If the unification timeline holds, the 2029 renegotiation will be the first commercial event for the new unified league — and the first multi-buyer, single-seller negotiation in Brazilian football history.** This is the structural inflection we identify in Section 3: rather than fragmented blocs negotiating separately with multiple buyers, a single unified league would have the leverage to maximise total rights value across the buyer universe. That said, the risk is real and worth flagging — bloc fragmentation could persist beyond 2029 if the unification process stalls, in which case the multi-buyer architecture of the current cycle could erode without a corresponding consolidation of selling power.

# Pillar #5 — Contract Architecture (2025-2029)

**The contracts that Libra and LFU signed for the 2025-2029 cycle are the first concrete output of the new market structure.** Instead of one dominant buyer (Globo) paying for everything, there are now five distinct buyers — each acquiring different types of rights through different distribution channels (broadcast TV, pay-TV, pay-per-view, streaming, and digital/YouTube). This pillar maps who paid what to whom. It also explains why all major contracts are synchronised to end in 2029, which makes that year the next critical inflection point for the market — particularly given the unification process described in Pillar #4.

### Total market size

**For the 2025-2029 cycle, the total contracted rights revenue across both blocs reaches approximately BRL 2.87bn per season** — with upside to roughly BRL 4.5bn if you include the indicative value of commercial advertising packages bundled into the contracts (often only partially realised). LFU captures the larger share, roughly 58% of the total, with Libra at 42%. **The market is now structurally a multi-buyer landscape**, spanning five distinct buyers: Globo (which signed separate contracts with both blocs), Amazon Prime Video, Record (a free-to-air TV network), and CazéTV/YouTube (a YouTube channel operated by Brazilian streamer Casimiro Miguel in partnership with LiveMode). **The implication is that the rights market has been fundamentally re-priced**: the multi-buyer structure has materially expanded the addressable pool versus the previous Globo-only architecture. In our view, this is the strongest evidence that the new architecture is structurally additive — not just redistributive.

### LFU contracts — the aggregate position

**LFU's combined contracted revenue for 2025 reaches approximately BRL 1.7bn per year, structured across four buyers.** First, Globo pays BRL 850mn per year to broadcast five matches per round of 12 LFU clubs on its main free-to-air channel. Second, Amazon Prime Video pays BRL 265mn in Year 1 (escalating over five years to approximately BRL 2bn cumulative) to stream one exclusive match per round to its Prime subscribers. Third, Record (a competing free-to-air network) pays BRL 200mn per year. Fourth, CazéTV/YouTube pays approximately BRL 250mn or more per year for OTT and digital rights. Notably, the Record and CazéTV/YouTube deals were both extended to 2029 in August 2025, synchronising the entire LFU contract stack to a common renegotiation window.

### Libra contracts — the concentrated position

**Libra adopted a more conservative strategy, betting on a single buyer rather than spreading rights across multiple platforms.** The bloc closed an exclusive deal with Globo at BRL 1.3bn per year, subsequently reduced to BRL 1.17bn following Corinthians' migration to LFU. The contract additionally includes a variable component: Libra receives 40% of net Premiere PPV revenue, which provides upside tied to how many Brazilian fans buy the premium subscription package each season. The contract spans 2025-2029, aligning with LFU's renegotiation window.

### LiveMode as broker

**Three of the four LFU contracts — Amazon, Record and CazéTV/YouTube — were intermediated by a Brazilian sports media company called LiveMode.** LiveMode has positioned itself as the de facto rights distribution platform for one half of the Brazilian football market, going well beyond the traditional role of a rights advisor. Importantly, this is especially relevant given that General Atlantic (a LFU investor, as discussed above) also holds a minority stake in LiveMode itself — creating a vertical alignment between league capital, distribution platform and rights buyers. LiveMode's broader mandate portfolio also includes the Paulistão (São Paulo state championship), FIFA tournaments (World Cup, Women's World Cup, Club World Cup), UEFA competitions (Euro, Europa League) and the Olympic Games.

### Globo's strategic response — repositioning, not retreat

**The narrative that "Globo lost the Brasileirão" misses the bigger picture.** Globo accepted to share exclusivity but **did not lose football revenue overall**: according to RD1, Globo's total football rights spend is reported to reach approximately BRL 5bn in 2026, up from ~BRL 2.5bn in 2025. The composition shifted, not the absolute size. The strategic moves underneath that number are worth tracking:

- **Brasileirão contracts** — Globo retains the largest packages in both Libra (BRL 1.17bn/year) and LFU (BRL 850mn/year), plus Premiere PPV upside via the 40% revenue share with Libra.
- **Copa do Brasil** — exclusive Globo contract until end-2026; the next cycle (2027-2030) is in active negotiation, with Globo facing its first credible competition from Disney/ESPN and CazéTV.
- **Estaduais (state championships)** — Globo dominates the regional championships outside São Paulo (Carioca, Mineiro, Cearense, Pernambucano, others). Paulistão is the notable exception, having been won by LiveMode — that case showed what a multi-buyer auction can deliver when the bloc is well-organised.
- **GeTV — the response to CazéTV** — Globo launched a new ad-supported digital sports channel positioned explicitly to compete with CazéTV. Same business model (free for viewers, ad-monetised), but with Globo's production infrastructure and rights portfolio behind it.
- **Globoplay Premium** — Globo's streaming tier now includes selected Brasileirão matches, designed to upsell pay-TV refugees who left Premiere/SporTV.

**In our view**, Globo's repositioning is consistent with how every legacy media incumbent has responded to digital disruption: keep the most valuable rights, monetise across multiple windows (broadcast + pay + PPV + streaming), and launch defensive products in the categories where you would otherwise lose share. **The risk for Globo** is that the next renegotiation cycles (Copa do Brasil 2027, Brasileirão 2029) will all happen under multi-buyer competition — and the share Globo captures could compress further. **The opportunity for Globo** is that it still has the production and distribution scale that no challenger has matched, which means it remains the bid floor on most rights packages even when it does not win them outright.

### 2029 — the synchronised renegotiation

**A subtle but important feature of the current cycle is that every major contract is set to expire at the end of 2029.** This synchronisation was not accidental: both blocs and the broadcasters preferred a common end date to align future negotiations. The practical implication is that 2029 will be the first time in Brazilian football history that a fully multi-buyer renegotiation happens simultaneously across the entire market — and potentially the first such renegotiation conducted by a unified league rather than two competing blocs (per the unification process in Pillar #4). **It will be a structural inflection point**: either the new architecture consolidates higher pricing (with multiple buyers bidding against a unified seller), or it reverts toward concentration if buyers retrench. Section 3 frames this as the anchor of our forward sizing scenarios.

# Pillar #6 — Revenue Distribution

**Selling the rights and bringing in the money is only half the story.** The other half is how that money gets divided among the clubs that make up each league. This is not a minor administrative detail — the distribution formula can make or break clubs, shape competitive dynamics on the pitch, and trigger the kind of political disputes that we just saw between Flamengo and Libra. **This pillar explains how each bloc divides the pie, why disputes have emerged, and how Brazil compares to other major leagues in terms of inequality between member clubs.**

### Libra criteria — 40/30/30

**Libra distributes contracted revenue among its member clubs using a three-component formula: 40% equal, 30% performance, 30% audience.** The 40% equal share is the "solidarity" component every team receives regardless of size or performance. The 30% performance share is allocated by final position in the league standings. The remaining 30% audience share is allocated by each club's share of paying Premiere subscribers, which is essentially a proxy for fan base size. Importantly, the audience component structurally favours clubs with larger pre-existing fanbases, creating a self-reinforcing concentration effect (more fans → more revenue → better squad → more fans). The contract base for distribution is BRL 1.17bn per year fixed (assuming at least 9 Libra clubs in Série A), plus 40% of net Premiere PPV revenue.

### LFU criteria — 45/30/25

**LFU adopted a modestly more egalitarian formula: 45% equal, 30% performance, 25% audience.** The 5-percentage-point reduction in the audience-weighted component compared with Libra is consistent with LFU's strategic positioning as the league for clubs that benefit from a flatter distribution. That said, in aggregate terms the difference is meaningful but not huge: a mid-sized club gets a slightly higher share of a slightly bigger pie (LFU's BRL 1.7bn pool vs Libra's BRL 1.17bn).

### What the actual distribution looks like

**The aggregate framework produces a real-world distribution that is even more skewed than the 5.7x global ratio suggests.** Galapagos Outfield 2024 data shows actual broadcasting revenue per club in Série A 2024, with Flamengo capturing roughly 8x what the smallest clubs receive — closer to a 1-club / 19-club asymmetry than a peer-comparable league.

**Top 5 clubs by 2024 broadcasting revenue (BRL mn):**

- Flamengo — **454**
- Corinthians — **295**
- Botafogo — **285**
- Atlético-MG — **248**
- Palmeiras — **244**

**Bottom 5 clubs by 2024 broadcasting revenue (BRL mn):**

- Cuiabá — **56**
- Athletico-PR — **58**
- Atlético-GO — **58**
- Criciúma — **60**
- RB Bragantino — **81**

**Key observations:**

- Top-to-bottom ratio: BRL 454mn ÷ BRL 56mn = **8.1x** — well above the 5.7x average commonly cited and significantly worse than the 1.0x ratio of NFL or La Liga
- The top 3 clubs (Flamengo, Corinthians, Botafogo) capture roughly **30% of all broadcasting revenue** across the 20-club Série A pool
- The "audience" component in distribution formulas explains why this ratio is more extreme than a flat "equal" share would produce — Flamengo's national fanbase concentrates Premiere subscribers, which compounds into a structurally larger 30% audience-weighted share
- Botafogo's 2024 position (3rd at BRL 285mn) reflects the SAF effect — once a mid-sized club, the Textor-controlled SAF won the Libertadores and accelerated Botafogo's earning power, particularly in the audience-weighted distribution

**In our view**, the actual distribution data is the most concrete evidence of why the unified-league discussion in Pillar #4 matters disproportionately for mid-tier clubs: they currently receive amounts that are commercially marginal (BRL 56-94mn/year), and the only way to materially expand their earning power is a flatter distribution formula at the unified-league level — exactly what LFU has advocated for since 2022.

### The Flamengo dispute — distribution under stress

**The 2025 Flamengo-Libra dispute illustrates how unstable a distribution model can become when a single dominant member challenges its terms.** Flamengo argued that the audience-calculation annex in the Libra rules lacked technical clarity, and that this was reducing its rightful share of the pool. The eventual settlement granted the club an incremental BRL 140-160mn per year through 2029, on top of the structural distribution — equivalent to roughly 12-14% of the bloc's total pool. **The dispute exposed two risks**: first, the audience methodology is contestable and could invite further legal or political challenges; and second, dominant members may seek private side-deals that effectively override the formal distribution rules.

### Brazil vs global peers — the persistent inequality gap

**Despite all the changes in regulation, governance and distribution formulas, Brazilian football remains structurally the most unequal major league in the world in terms of revenue distribution.** Data from Galapagos Outfield 2024 shows the top-to-bottom revenue ratio in the Brasileirão at 5.7x — meaning the biggest club earns roughly 5.7 times what the smallest club earns each year. For comparison: the ratio is 2.9x in Italy's Serie A, 1.5x in the Premier League, and broadly 1.0x in La Liga, the NBA and the NFL (the latter three operate revenue-sharing models that approach full equality). Both Libra and LFU have nominally egalitarian components in their formulas, but the audience-weighted portion preserves much of the existing inequality. **In our view, the 2029 renegotiation — conducted under the unified-league framework (Pillar #4) — will be the most direct opportunity to narrow this gap structurally.**

# SECTION 2 — LiveMode: A Case Study in Sports Rights Distribution

**LiveMode is the central commercial intermediary of the new Brazilian sports rights market — which is why it deserves a dedicated section here.** Founded in São Paulo in 2017, it holds the rights commercialization mandate for the LFU bloc (26 Brasileirão clubs), the FIFA 2026 World Cup in Brazil, the Tokyo 2020 and Paris 2024 Olympics, plus the Paulistão, UEFA Euro and Europa League, Pan American Games and the women's Copa Libertadores. It also operates **CazéTV**, the YouTube-native sports channel co-founded with streamer Casimiro Miguel in 2022 and fully acquired in November 2025. **Backed by General Atlantic and XP Private Equity since April 2024 at an implied valuation of ~BRL 2bn, LiveMode embodies the new logic of the market: a vertical operator that wins as rights fragment and audiences move to digital.**

**Our deep dive follows the company's strategic evolution.** We start with origin — the same two founders who built and sold Esporte Interativo in 2015 are the architects behind LiveMode today, and the continuity of operator team is what differentiates the company from a typical sports agency. From there we unpack the business model: an unusual combination of rights broker, content producer and platform operator that reinforces itself in ways pure agencies cannot replicate. We then zoom into CazéTV — the most visible asset in the portfolio, and the case study at the heart of Brazil's digital sports migration. The ownership structure follows: General Atlantic, XP Private Equity and (more recently) Cristiano Ronaldo as shareholders, with implied valuation in the BRL 2bn range. We inventory the rights portfolio LiveMode currently controls — from the LFU Brasileirão mandate to FIFA 2026 — and close with the international ambition that frames the next chapter.

# Pillar #1 — Origin: From Esporte Interativo to LiveMode (2003-2017)

**To understand LiveMode, we need to start a decade earlier.** The same two entrepreneurs who built and sold Brazil's most successful sports media platform of the 2000s are the ones who founded LiveMode in 2017. The continuity is important: LiveMode is not a start-up, it is the second act of an experienced operator team that learned from a previous cycle and applied those lessons to the next one. This pillar traces that arc.

### TopSports and Esporte Interativo (2003-2015)

**In 2003, two Brazilian entrepreneurs — Edgar Diniz and Sérgio Lopes — founded TopSports, the company that would launch the Esporte Interativo (EI) brand.** EI grew into one of the leading sports media operators in Brazil, eventually distributing UEFA Champions League and other premium football rights on free-to-air TV. It pioneered the first Brazilian live sports broadcast on Facebook (2011) and launched the first sports-dedicated OTT platform in Latin America (2012). In 2015, the founders sold EI to Turner Broadcasting (now part of WarnerMedia, the second-largest media group in the world at the time) in what was one of the most relevant transactions in Brazilian sports media history. **Deal economics**: Turner had been a minority shareholder since 2013, when it acquired 20% of EI for approximately BRL 80mn. In January 2015, it bought the remaining 80% in a transaction estimated at roughly **BRL 400mn (~USD 150mn at the time)** — combining ~BRL 250mn in equity for the outgoing shareholders plus additional commitments in infrastructure, headcount and rights acquisitions. The EI brand was eventually deactivated in 2018, following the AT&T/Time Warner merger that consolidated Turner under WarnerMedia. **In our view**, the exit gives Diniz and Lopes a documented track record at meaningful scale — and helps explain why institutional investors (notably General Atlantic) were willing to back their second venture at premium terms.

### The founding bet (2017)

**After a transition period at Turner, Diniz and Lopes left and used the proceeds — plus contacts made during a stint in Silicon Valley — to start LiveMode in 2017 from a co-working space.** The thesis was straightforward: rights holders increasingly needed help reaching audiences across fragmented platforms (YouTube, Twitch, Prime Video, traditional TV), and existing agencies were not built for that complexity. The bet was that the same forces that had created EI's opportunity (premium content on linear TV) would now create the next opportunity in digital distribution. **In our view**, the track record of a successful prior exit applied to the next cycle is what distinguishes LiveMode from a typical sports rights agency: the company is not learning the business, it is replaying a thesis with sharper tools — and that operating premium, in our view, is reflected in the institutional capital it has attracted.

# Pillar #2 — Business Model: Rights Agent, Producer, and Platform Operator

**LiveMode does three things that traditional rights agents do not.** First, it sells rights on behalf of leagues and federations — a traditional brokerage role. Second, it produces broadcast content itself, including live commentary, graphics, and digital-first formats. Third, it operates direct-to-consumer platforms (most visibly CazéTV) where it monetises audiences via sponsored content rather than subscriptions. **The combination makes LiveMode less an agent and more a vertically integrated rights operator** — and is the structural reason why it has been able to win mandates that pure brokerages cannot.

### The brokerage role

**LiveMode acts as the exclusive media rights agent for major properties spanning football, FIFA tournaments, UEFA competitions and the Olympic Games.** Its portfolio includes the Paulistão (São Paulo state championship), FIFA tournaments (Men's and Women's World Cup, Club World Cup), UEFA Euro and Europa League, Pan American Games, the Olympic Games, and the LFU bloc covering 26 Brazilian clubs. As broker, it negotiates with multiple buyers simultaneously — Globo, Amazon, Record, YouTube, ESPN — to maximise total rights value across the rights stack rather than relying on a single counterparty.

### Content production

**The company produces its own live broadcasts with editorial teams, commentary booths and digital production capabilities.** This means LiveMode can deliver complete coverage even when distributing via lean digital channels like YouTube — without depending on a traditional broadcaster's production infrastructure. Importantly, the capability is essential for properties like the Olympics, where it covered 800 hours of programming during Paris 2024.

### Platform operation

**Through CazéTV (and by extension via YouTube and Twitch), LiveMode operates direct-to-consumer audience platforms.** The business model is ad-supported: viewers watch for free, advertisers pay for sponsored content slots and brand integrations. This is closer to a traditional broadcaster's model than to streaming subscription — and it works because the audience scale is genuinely large, as we describe in the next pillar. **In our view**, the vertical integration of broker + producer + platform is the durable moat: each link reinforces the others (rights win → audience scale → ad pricing → more rights wins), and competitors offering only one of the three cannot replicate the loop without substantial capital and time.

### Competitive positioning — global peers and the Brazilian field

**LiveMode operates in a market segment with clear global peers but limited Brazilian competition at scale.** The closest international comparables are sports rights agencies and media operators with the same broker + production + distribution mix: **TEAM Marketing AG** (UEFA Champions League commercial rights), **Infront** (DAZN's parent network), **Endeavor / IMG** (the most prominent global operator, combining rights agency, talent management and event production), **CAA Sports** (US-focused), and historically **Lagardère Sports**. In the Brazilian market, the closest peer is **Wolff Sports & Marketing**, which dominates football club-level sponsorships but does not operate distribution platforms or hold first-party rights mandates at LiveMode's scale.

**LiveMode itself positions the company as a "partner/JV with sports entities" rather than a traditional agency.** This is a meaningful differentiation: traditional rights agents are compensated on commission and have no skin in the game beyond the deal cycle; LiveMode increasingly co-invests in the production and the commercial outcome, which aligns incentives with the leagues and federations it serves. In April 2024, LiveMode became **the only Brazilian company listed in a global ranking of most attractive sports media companies** (MKT Esportivo) — an external confirmation that the positioning is being recognised internationally and is consistent with what we believe the General Atlantic investment thesis is built around.

# Pillar #3 — CazéTV: From Partnership to 100% Acquisition

**CazéTV is LiveMode's most visible asset and, arguably, the single biggest case study in Brazilian sports media of the past five years.** It was created in 2022 as a partnership between LiveMode and Casimiro Miguel, one of Brazil's most popular streamers — originally based on Twitch with a large football-following audience. The original ownership structure was 51% LiveMode and 49% Casimiro's CMiguel Produções. The channel debuted at the 2022 FIFA World Cup in Qatar and gained mainstream visibility at the Paris 2024 Olympics, where it set Brazilian YouTube viewership records. **In November 2025, LiveMode bought out Casimiro's 49% stake — taking 100% control of the CazéTV brand and making him a shareholder of the global LiveMode holding instead.**

### The partnership era (2022-2025)

**CazéTV launched in 2022 as the digital home of Casimiro Miguel's sports coverage.** With the FIFA World Cup in Qatar as the first major event, the channel went mainstream rapidly. The format combines live broadcasting with Casimiro's commentary style, attracting a younger audience that traditional broadcasters had struggled to reach. The original split (51% LiveMode, 49% Casimiro's production company) reflected the partnership logic: LiveMode brought the rights, infrastructure and capital; Casimiro brought the audience and brand.

### Paris 2024 — the breakthrough

**At the Paris 2024 Olympics, CazéTV reached 41 million devices on YouTube, with an average of 650,000 viewers per minute during live competition broadcasts and 800 hours of programming.** The channel gained 2 million new YouTube subscribers in two weeks (taking the total to 16.2 million) and 4 million new Instagram followers. On peak days, a single day reached 71.9 million people on the CazéTV YouTube channel; total Olympics watch-time reached 105.31 million hours — the bulk of all online viewership for the Games globally. **This was the moment CazéTV stopped being a streamer-led experiment and became a serious competitor to traditional broadcast**, prompting Globo to publicly change strategy to defend against it.

**That scale has proven durable, not one-off.** Through full-year 2025, CazéTV registered **3.7 billion YouTube views** and ranked as the **#1 destination for live sports content on YouTube globally** — a Brazilian channel leading a global category. **11 of YouTube's 16 largest live broadcasts in history occurred on CazéTV** — a record ahead of any traditional broadcaster's digital arm internationally. **In our view**, this is what differentiates the LiveMode investment thesis from a domestic sports media play: CazéTV is not just the largest Brazilian sports YouTube channel — it is the largest live sports YouTube channel in the world, and that category leadership translates directly into bargaining power with both rights holders and advertisers.

### Monetisation scale — sponsor revenue and brand portfolio

**The CazéTV economic engine is closer to a tier-1 broadcaster than to a YouTube channel.** Industry data on major event cycles shows commercial revenue in the billion-real range — orders of magnitude above what a typical streaming sports channel monetises. The key numbers:

- **Brasileirão 2026 (CazéTV + YouTube package)**: 10 confirmed master sponsors at approximately **BRL 185mn each, totaling a ~BRL 1.85bn sponsor pool** (Adtrend / industry reporting).
- **FIFA World Cup 2026**: estimated **>BRL 2bn in projected sponsorship revenue** (Terra), reflecting the favourable Brazilian time-zone exposure (matches in afternoon/prime time given US/Canada/Mexico hosting).
- **FIFA World Cup 2022 (Qatar) sponsors**: Ambev, Bet365, Betnacional, Coca-Cola, Decolar, GM, iFood, Itaú, KTO, Mercado Livre, Vivo — a near-complete blue-chip Brazilian advertiser roster.
- **Paris 2024 Olympics sponsors**: Corona, Esportes da Sorte, Hellmann's, iFood, Visa (with Banco do Brasil), Vivo and Volkswagen.
- **Brand ROI evidence**: iFood reported **2.8 million orders generated during the Qatar World Cup** coverage on CazéTV — the kind of attributable conversion metric that traditional broadcasters cannot easily produce.

**In our view**, the sponsorship economics are what differentiate CazéTV from the broader "creator economy" or typical sports streaming platforms: the channel commands tier-1 brand budgets, runs full-funnel commercial campaigns, and generates measurable ROI for advertisers. This is the lever that justifies LiveMode's BRL ~2bn implied valuation (developed in Pillar #4) — not just the audience numbers, but the dollar-per-impression that the channel converts those audiences into.

### The 100% acquisition (November 2025)

**In November 2025, LiveMode acquired the remaining 49% of CazéTV, taking 100% control of the brand.** Casimiro Miguel exchanged his CazéTV equity for a shareholding in LiveMode Cayman, the international holding company that controls all of LiveMode's operations. The deal made Casimiro a partner in the broader LiveMode platform rather than the owner of a single brand — aligning his incentives with the group's international expansion. The financial terms were not publicly disclosed, but the strategic logic was clear: CazéTV had become large enough that integration into the parent holding was more valuable than continued partnership structure.

# Pillar #4 — Institutional Capital and Shareholders

**LiveMode's transformation from a Brazilian agency into a global platform was funded by institutional capital.** In April 2024, General Atlantic — the US-based global growth equity firm — led a strategic minority investment in LiveMode, with participation from XP Private Equity (the private equity arm of XP Investimentos). The deal was announced as a long-term strategic partnership rather than a financial-only investment, with General Atlantic providing access to its global network of sports rights holders, broadcasters and operating expertise. Total funding raised by LiveMode is approximately USD 85mn (PitchBook), with a registered capital base of BRL 261.6mn. **Notably, in 2025 Cristiano Ronaldo joined the shareholder base** — a deal that was widely reported but with terms not publicly disclosed.

### General Atlantic and XP — the April 2024 strategic investment

**General Atlantic invests globally in growth-stage businesses and has been increasing its sports media exposure across markets.** The investment in LiveMode was structured as a minority strategic stake with operational and network value (in addition to capital). XP Private Equity participated alongside, reflecting the local relationship and the parallel investment that XP and General Atlantic were making in LFU. Importantly, this is the same General Atlantic that holds a minority stake in LFU itself — meaning the same capital backs both the league (rights seller) and the agency (rights distributor), an unusual vertical alignment that is worth flagging. **We read the GA cross-holding as the single most important signal in the Brazilian sports media market**: it tells us that the smartest global capital deploying into Brazilian football believes the distribution layer (LiveMode) is at least as valuable as the league layer (LFU) — and possibly more, because the platform monetises across many properties while a single league monetises only one.

### Capital structure and disclosed funding

**LiveMode has disclosed approximately USD 85mn in total funding (PitchBook), with a registered capital base of BRL 261.6mn (Brazilian corporate registry).** Specific revenue, EBITDA and current valuation are not publicly disclosed — LiveMode remains a private company with no obligation to file detailed financials. **However, press reporting around the April 2024 round provides a meaningful valuation anchor**: Brazil Journal estimated that General Atlantic and XP invested approximately **BRL 440mn for an aggregate stake above 20% of LiveMode**, which implies a post-money valuation of roughly **BRL 2bn (~USD 400mn at the time of the deal)**. Neither LiveMode, General Atlantic nor XP confirmed these figures officially, but the estimate has been widely cited and is consistent with the company's disclosed capital base, the breadth of its rights portfolio, and the volume of activity (~1,000 live broadcasts in 2024 across CazéTV and third-party clients). **In our view**, the BRL 2bn implied valuation is defensible if the unified-league hypothesis (Section 1, Pillar #4) materialises — and would prove conservative if international expansion (Pillar #6) delivers.

### Cristiano Ronaldo as shareholder (2025)

**In late 2025, Cristiano Ronaldo became a LiveMode shareholder.** The investment is reportedly part of LiveMode's international expansion strategy — Ronaldo brings global brand visibility and entertainment-industry networks that align with LiveMode's stated ambition to scale outside Brazil. The deal also reinforces the message that LiveMode is no longer a purely Brazilian agency.

# Pillar #5 — Rights Portfolio: What LiveMode Actually Controls

**LiveMode's rights portfolio in 2025-2026 spans three categories that the company explicitly describes as its three strategic pillars: Football Leagues, Olympic Sports, and Emerging Sports.** The most commercially relevant assets are the LFU mandate (26 clubs for the Brasileirão 2025-2029), the FIFA 2026 Men's World Cup rights for Brazil (renewed mandate), and the Olympic Games broadcasting rights through CazéTV. The portfolio strategy is consistent: control properties that are large enough to anchor advertiser interest, frequent enough to sustain audience engagement, and synced with the platform's distribution capability.

### LFU — the Brasileirão mandate

**LiveMode is the exclusive rights agent for LFU, covering 26 clubs across Série A, B and C.** It intermediated three of the four LFU broadcast deals for 2025-2029: Amazon Prime Video (BRL 265mn Y1, approximately BRL 2bn total over five years), Record (BRL 200mn/year), and CazéTV/YouTube (~BRL 250mn+/year). Total LFU rights revenue intermediated by LiveMode is approximately BRL 850mn-1bn per year — the core of the company's commercial base.

### FIFA 2026 World Cup — the renewed mandate

LiveMode has confirmed it will sell rights across open TV, pay-TV and streaming, with roughly half of the 104 games available as exclusive packages. Through CazéTV, it has also acquired the next three editions of the women's Copa Libertadores from CONMEBOL — extending its portfolio into women's football, which is currently underserved by broadcasters but growing fast.

# Pillar #6 — International Ambition

**LiveMode has explicitly stated international expansion as a strategic priority, leveraging General Atlantic's global network and the CazéTV brand.** As of 2025, the company operates an international holding structure (LiveMode Cayman) and has begun acquiring rights mandates beyond Brazil. The thesis is straightforward: the same shifts that created the Brazilian rights opportunity (regulatory unbundling, OTT/digital migration, fragmented distribution) are also happening in other Latin American markets and beyond. **LiveMode wants to be the platform that takes Brazilian-developed playbooks (free-to-viewer, ad-supported, multi-platform distribution) to those markets first.**

### LiveMode Cayman — the international holding

**The 2025 reorganisation that brought Casimiro into the group also formalised the international corporate structure.** LiveMode Cayman is the top-of-house holding that owns the operating subsidiaries across geographies. Notably, this signals that international expansion is now operationally underway rather than aspirational.

### General Atlantic as global network multiplier

**General Atlantic's global presence (Europe, US, Asia, Middle East) was cited explicitly in the April 2024 investment announcement as an enabler of LiveMode's international expansion.** The fund's portfolio of sports and media assets across markets gives LiveMode warm introductions and operating intelligence that a purely Brazilian agency would not have access to.

### What to watch

**Three signals will indicate whether the international thesis is working**: (i) rights mandates for non-Brazilian properties (the first will likely be in Latin America); (ii) CazéTV-style platform launches in other markets; and (iii) M&A activity if LiveMode acquires existing local players to accelerate scale. That said, as of May 2026 the company has not announced any of the three publicly — the international thesis is still being built, not delivered.

# SECTION 3 — Sizing the Brazilian Sports Rights Opportunity

The market we described in Section 1 is mid-transition: regulatory unbundling is done, the multi-buyer architecture is in place, but the first repricing cycle (2025-2029) is just beginning. **Our goal in this section is to size the opportunity** — to help investors understand how large the Brazilian football broadcasting rights market is today, how it compares to global peers, and where it might realistically be heading by the time the 2029 renegotiation cycle is ready to be priced.

**Our sizing follows five steps.** We start with the current baseline — total Série A revenue, contracted rights value, CBF financials, and the broader football economy that surrounds them. We then trace the audience trajectory underneath the numbers: Brazil's accelerating cord-cutting and the digital migration that is reshaping where value flows. With baseline and trend established, we put Brazil in global perspective by comparing it to the Premier League, La Liga and Ligue 1. We then identify the three vectors that could materially expand the pool over the next cycle (international rights, sports betting integration, and new OTT entrants) and the two risks that could compress it (monetisation lag and CADE intervention). We close with three forward sizing scenarios — base, bull and bear — for the 2029-2034 cycle.

We frame this as a "first-principles sizing" rather than a precise forecast: there are too many moving variables, and our goal is to inform investment thinking rather than to deliver point estimates.

<a id="s3p1"></a>

# Pillar #1 — The 2025 Baseline

**To understand where the market can go, we need to first anchor where it is.** The Brazilian football economy in 2024-2025 is materially larger than most international investors realise, but still trails global peers by an order of magnitude when measured strictly on broadcast rights. The total Série A revenue pool reached BRL 10.2bn in 2024 (Sports Value / Galapagos), of which BRL 3.3bn came from broadcasting rights — about one third of the total. The 2025 contracted rights value across Libra and LFU is approximately BRL 2.87bn per year, with upside to BRL 4.5bn including commercial tabela value. For the broader football ecosystem, the CBF reported BRL 1.5bn in 2024 revenue (record, +17.6%), with BRL 723.9mn from broadcasting and commercial rights at the confederation level (separate from club-level rights).

### Total Brazilian football economy

**Sports Value pegs Série A revenue at BRL 10.2bn in 2024 — a record year for the industry.** Marketing revenue grew 36% to BRL 1.9bn, matchday revenue rose 22% to BRL 1.1bn, and member-subscription (sócio-torcedor) revenue rose 17% past BRL 850mn. Broadcasting rights at BRL 3.3bn represented roughly 32% of the total. Importantly, the aggregate debt stock of Série A clubs reached BRL 14.6bn in the same year — a critical financial vulnerability that makes the rights revenue trajectory existentially important for the industry. **Put differently**: the Série A debt stock implies that even a 10% compression in rights revenue would meaningfully impair the financial fragility of mid-tier clubs. In our view, this is why the bloc dynamics in Section 1 are not just commercial but increasingly systemic — and why the unification timeline matters financially as well as strategically.

### Brasileirão broadcasting rights (2025)

**The Brasileirão 2025-2029 contracted rights stack across LFU and Libra reaches approximately BRL 2.87bn per year (LFU BRL 1.7bn + Libra BRL 1.17bn).** Including the 40% Premiere PPV revenue share that Libra captures variably, plus commercial advertising tabela value attributed to the contracts, the upper bound estimate reaches BRL 4.5bn. This is the baseline against which all forward scenarios are measured.

### CBF as a reference layer

**The CBF reported BRL 1.5bn in 2024 revenue (record, +17.6% YoY), with BRL 723.9mn from broadcasting and commercial rights.** Total CBF investment in football reached BRL 1.18bn in 2025 (+9% YoY) and CBF cash position reached BRL 2.4bn (+134%). Notably, the CBF layer is separate from club-level rights but provides a useful cross-check on the total economy and on the confederation's role as a centralised rights aggregator for national team competitions.

# Pillar #2 — The Audience Trajectory: Cord-Cutting and Digital Migration

**The most important structural fact about the Brazilian sports media market is that the audience is leaving traditional pay-TV at speed.** ANCINE's 2024 yearbook reports that pay-TV subscriber numbers fell 22.4% in 2024 alone, reaching 8.1 million accesses. This is a continuation of a longer trajectory — from a peak of roughly 19 million subscribers around 2014, the base has contracted by more than half in a decade. Importantly, the migration is not away from sports content but toward digital and OTT platforms: Brazil's OTT sports streaming market is now valued at USD 1.0-1.5bn (Ken Research) and growing. **The implication for rights pricing is that the value pool is not shrinking; it is reshaping.** Buyers like Amazon Prime, YouTube/CazéTV and ad-supported streaming services capture what traditional pay-TV is losing.

### Pay-TV in steep decline

**ANCINE Anuário 2024 reports pay-TV accesses fell 22.4% to 8.1 million in 2024 — the steepest single-year drop in over a decade.** This compresses the addressable market for Premiere (Globo's PPV) and SporTV (Globo's pay-TV channel), the two pillars of the Globo monetisation model on football. The continued decline directly compresses subscriber-driven monetisation in those windows.

### OTT and streaming filling the gap

**Brazil's OTT sports streaming market is valued at USD 1.0-1.5bn (Ken Research), growing at low-to-mid teens annually.** Amazon Prime, ESPN+/Disney+, Star+, YouTube and CazéTV are all increasingly relevant for sports rights monetisation. Notably, the largest single OTT football contract in 2025 — Amazon Prime's BRL 265mn Y1 deal with LFU — would have been inconceivable in the previous (2020-2024) cycle.

### Why this matters for rights pricing

**The audience trajectory means traditional broadcasters (Globo) will face increasing pressure on the marginal rights they can profitably buy in the next cycle, while digital entrants will face increasing willingness to pay.** If the audience continues to migrate at current rates, the 2029 renegotiation could see a meaningful share shift from traditional bundle (FTA + pay-TV + PPV) toward streaming and OTT — with corresponding implications for rights pricing power.

# Pillar #3 — Brazil vs Global Comparables

**Brazil's broadcasting rights market is structurally smaller than European Big 5 leagues, but the gap is narrower than most investors assume — and it is narrowing further.** The Premier League's 2025-29 cycle is worth approximately USD 16.5bn total (~BRL 80bn at current FX), with the international component (USD 8.7bn) now larger than domestic. La Liga's 2027-32 domestic cycle was just announced at EUR 6.1bn (~USD 7.2bn). The Brasileirão's 2025-29 cycle at BRL 2.87bn/year (~BRL 14.4bn cumulative, ~USD 2.9bn at current FX) is therefore roughly 20-25% of La Liga and ~18% of the Premier League — but consistent enough with French Ligue 1 that one industry source (SportBusiness) believes Brazil could overtake France as the 5th-most-valuable football league by domestic rights. **That said, the structural opportunity remains international rights monetisation, where Brazil is essentially a greenfield.**

### Premier League — the benchmark and its plateau

**The Premier League's 2025-29 cycle reached USD 16.48bn total (+17% versus the prior cycle).** However, the domestic component grew only 2.3% annually (barely ahead of inflation), and per-match value actually dropped 24% versus the 2016-19 peak. The real growth is international: international rights are now USD 8.74bn, exceeding domestic for the first time, and have grown roughly 10x since 2007.

**The trajectory matters more than the absolute scale.** The Premier League was founded on 20 February 1992, when the "Big Five" (Manchester United, Liverpool, Arsenal, Everton, Tottenham) led the break from the English Football League. The first broadcasting deal — with Sky and the BBC — was worth £304mn over five years, the largest in British sports history at the time. Thirty-three years later, the same league commands £6.7bn per cycle on domestic alone — a 22x nominal uplift (~10x in real terms after inflation). **In our view**, this trajectory matters to Brazil for two reasons: first, it shows what a well-run, independent club-owned league can achieve when broadcasters compete properly; second, it shows that the largest gains come well after initial formation — Brazil today is in something analogous to England in 1995-2000, with the structural change in place but the financial gains still ahead.

### La Liga — pacing growth

**La Liga locked in EUR 6.135bn (~USD 7.2bn) for the 2027-2032 domestic cycle — a 9% uplift over the previous deal, slightly faster than inflation but below the Premier League trajectory.** Notably, La Liga's international rights story is much weaker than the Premier League's, which is a useful cautionary signal for Brazil.

### Bundesliga — €1.121bn/year, modest growth

**The Bundesliga (Germany) secured €1.121bn per season in domestic media rights for the 2025-26 to 2028-29 cycle**, distributed across the 36 clubs in Bundesliga 1 and 2 (per DFL). The new deal represents a 2% per-season increase over the prior cycle — roughly €21mn additional per season — which means the German market is essentially flat in real terms after inflation. Sky Deutschland and DAZN are the principal buyers.

### Serie A — €900mn/year, locked through 2029

**Italy's Serie A secured a minimum €4.5bn over five years for the 2024-2029 cycle, equivalent to ~€900mn per season.** The deal is with incumbent partners DAZN and Sky Italia. The result is broadly stable versus the prior cycle and confirms Serie A's position as the third-largest European league by domestic rights, behind only the Premier League and La Liga.

### Ligue 1 — the cautionary tale

**Ligue 1 (France) is the cautionary case for what happens when domestic broadcasters underperform.** The LFP entered the 2024-2029 cycle targeting €1bn per season, but no satisfactory bids materialised. The league salvaged a last-minute deal with DAZN (€400mn/year) and beIN Sports (€100mn/year), totalling just €500mn per season. Worse: **DAZN triggered an exit clause in 2025**, citing disappointing subscriber numbers, paying a €100mn exit fee — leaving Ligue 1 without a primary domestic broadcast partner. **In our view**, the Ligue 1 collapse is the real-world embodiment of the "monetisation lag" risk we discuss in Pillar #4: a structural mismatch between rights ambitions and what single broadcasters can sustainably monetise. Brazil's multi-buyer architecture distributes that risk across five buyers rather than concentrating it on one.

### MLS (United States) — the streaming-only benchmark

**Major League Soccer signed a $2.5bn 10-year exclusive deal with Apple in 2022**, equivalent to ~$250mn per season distributed entirely through Apple TV's MLS Season Pass. The deal eliminated traditional broadcast distribution. In 2025, Apple and MLS agreed to revised terms that bring the contract to end in 2029. **For Brazil**, MLS is the most relevant streaming-only case study: a smaller league than the Brasileirão by fan base and by rights value, yet it monetised through a single-platform streaming deal. Amazon's BRL 265mn Y1 deal with LFU is conceptually similar (single-platform streaming exclusive) and suggests that streaming-only models can scale further in Brazil.

### IPL Cricket — the emerging-market benchmark

**The Indian Premier League is the fastest-growing major sports property globally**: its domestic and international rights packages reached approximately $6.4bn in the current cycle, making IPL the second-most-valuable sports property in the world (behind only the NFL). For Brazil, IPL is the most relevant emerging-market benchmark: a non-European league in a developing economy that scaled to global top-tier rights values within a single decade. **In our view**, IPL's trajectory shows what an emerging-market football league with proper distribution architecture could achieve over a 10-15 year horizon.

### Brazil — already at European Big 5 parity

**The combined Libra and LFU domestic rights deals reach roughly BRL 2.87bn per year — equivalent to approximately EUR 470mn at current FX.** Put against the comparables above, this places Brazil essentially at Ligue 1 levels (EUR 500mn/year, and in distress after the DAZN exit) and on the same order of magnitude as Serie A a cycle ago. SportBusiness has flagged that Brazil could already qualify as the 5th-most-valuable domestic-rights football market globally. **The relevant framing has shifted: Brazil is no longer chasing the Big 5 — it has arrived at the bottom of that pack, and the question for the 2029 cycle is whether the market pushes it higher rather than whether it can break in.**

### Where the structural gap remains

**International rights monetisation is the persistent gap.** The Brasileirão's international rights generate close to zero today, versus USD 8.7bn for the Premier League. Even modest international monetisation would add meaningful upside to the 2029 renegotiation envelope — and this is the single biggest forward catalyst we identify in the rest of this section.

# Pillar #4 — Growth Vectors and Risks to 2029

**Looking forward to the 2029 renegotiation cycle, three vectors could materially expand the rights pool — and two risks could compress it.** The vectors are: (i) international rights monetisation, which is essentially zero today; (ii) sports betting integration, which has just become regulated in Brazil and is reshaping advertising economics; and (iii) further OTT platform competition, particularly if new global streamers (Netflix, Apple TV, DAZN, ByteDance) enter the bidding. The risks are: (i) audience fragmentation that exceeds advertiser willingness to follow (audiences migrate faster than monetisation models adapt); and (ii) bloc consolidation pressure from CADE, which has already barred new member additions during ongoing investigation. We discuss each below.

### Vector 1 — International rights

**The Brasileirão's international rights generate essentially nothing today.** Importantly, following recent media reports (ESPN, February 2025), Libra and LFU agreed to jointly negotiate international rights without Flamengo — the first time the two blocs cooperated on anything. The economic potential is meaningful given the multi-billion-dollar scale of comparable international rights pools at peer leagues (such as the Premier League's USD 8.74bn per cycle), but the magnitude of upside for Brazil depends on negotiation outcomes that have not yet been priced.

### Vector 2 — Sports betting integration

**Brazil legalised online sports betting in 2024, and operators (Bet365, Sportingbet, Estrela Bet, Esportes da Sorte, KTO) have rapidly become the largest advertisers in Brazilian football.** According to Galapagos, master sponsorship value across Série A clubs went from BRL 579mn in 2024 to BRL 988mn in 2025 — a 70% uplift in a single year, largely driven by betting operators. Notably, rights deals increasingly include betting-related inventory, and the monetisation potential is still maturing.

### Vector 3 — New OTT entrants

**The current OTT participation (Amazon, CazéTV/YouTube, Record on FTA) is broad but not yet saturated.** Netflix is exploring sports rights globally; Apple TV has done MLS and MLB deals in the US; DAZN is active in Europe and Latin America; ByteDance/TikTok could plausibly enter. Any of these joining the 2029 bidding would meaningfully expand the buyer pool and create additional pricing pressure.

### Risk 1 — Monetisation lag (the Ligue 1 cautionary tale)

**The fastest scenario for audience migration could outpace advertiser/subscription monetisation.** If audiences move to digital faster than monetisation models catch up, the rights pool could compress as buyers retrench from over-paying. **Ligue 1 is the real-world embodiment of this risk**: the French league targeted EUR 1bn/year for its 2024-2029 cycle, ultimately settled for EUR 500mn, and then watched DAZN exit mid-contract in 2025 after paying a EUR 100mn break fee. In our view, this is the principal risk to the bull case — and one that the Brazilian market is not immune to if Premiere's subscriber decline accelerates without commensurate growth from Amazon, CazéTV and other OTT entrants.

### Risk 2 — CADE regulatory pressure

**In late 2025, CADE barred new club additions to both Libra and LFU pending investigation into potential anticompetitive behaviour.** Libra paid a BRL 559k fine for gun-jumping. That said, if CADE eventually forces structural changes — for example, requiring a unified single-bloc negotiation — the multi-buyer architecture could be partially undone, with uncertain implications for pricing power.

# Pillar #5 — Forward Outlook: Three Scenarios for the 2029-2034 Cycle

**For our forward outlook, we describe three qualitative scenarios for the 2029-2034 rights cycle — base, bull and bear.** Specific magnitude estimates are left for later modelling work; at this stage, we focus on the structural mechanisms that need to hold for each scenario to materialise.

### Base case — the trajectory continues

**Domestic rights uplift broadly in line with mature European league trajectories** (low-single-digit annual growth, modestly ahead of inflation). International rights begin to ramp but remain a fraction of domestic. Sports betting integration continues to expand the commercial pool. No new OTT entrant materially shifts the bidding dynamic. The unified-league process consolidates seller leverage at the margin but does not fully replace the current bloc structure.

### Bull case — multi-buyer competition accelerates

**A new OTT entrant (Netflix, Apple TV or equivalent) joins the bidding seriously.** International rights ramp meaningfully, capturing a non-trivial share of what comparable leagues realise on the global stage. Sports betting becomes fully embedded as a rights monetisation channel rather than a sponsorship-only relationship. The unified-league hypothesis materialises in time for the 2029 negotiation and consolidates the seller side against multiple buyers.

### Bear case — fragmentation persists, monetisation stalls

**CADE forces restructuring back toward single-bloc negotiation or otherwise undoes the multi-buyer architecture.** Globo retrenches on rights spend as the Premiere subscriber base continues to compress without a viable digital replacement. Amazon, Record or CazéTV reduce or do not renew at current levels. Sports betting regulation tightens. The unification process stalls.

### Implications for investment thesis

**The asymmetry favours the bull case in our view, but the risks are real and worth monitoring.** The base case alone supports a positive narrative on Brazilian football rights, even before considering the structural upside from international monetisation and bloc consolidation. The next critical inflection is the 2029 renegotiation; the months immediately before (Q3-Q4 2028) will be when the new contract architecture becomes visible. Our recommendation is to use the next 18 months to position ahead of that visibility window. **In our view**, the highest-conviction angle is the distribution layer (LiveMode and adjacent platforms) rather than the league layer or the clubs themselves: it captures the multi-buyer economics regardless of which buyer wins, scales with audience migration rather than against it, and is structurally more capital-efficient than owning rights or owning clubs.
